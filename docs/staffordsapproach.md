# Stafford's Approach to Short-Form Content for Artists

**Source:** Podcast — "How To Make A Sh*t Load of Content to Promote Your Music ft. Patrick Stafford" (One More Time Podcast)  
**Subject:** Patrick Stafford — videographer, content strategist, and worldbuilder who works hands-on with artists to shoot high-quality promo content in record time.

---

## The Core Philosophy

Stafford's entire system rests on a few foundational beliefs:

1. **Volume is the strategy.** You can't out-plan your way to success — you outwork it. Most artists overthink and under-produce. The market rewards the people who just do more.
2. **Content is an extension of the art, not separate from it.** The discomfort artists feel about content comes from treating it as something foreign. It's the same as making the music — you communicate, you put it out.
3. **The song is the real product.** A great song with a bad reel still gets results. A bad song with amazing content won't convert fans. The music has to be undeniable. Content amplifies — it doesn't replace.
4. **The opportunity is wide open.** Most people won't do the work. Attention is the scarcest currency and most artists leave it on the table.

---

## Step 1: Pick the Emotion

**Before anything else:** In 1–2 words, what does the song *feel like?*

- Heartbreak. Confidence. Nostalgia. Rage. Joy. Freedom.
- This one word becomes your **content filter for everything** — every decision about location, lighting, energy, framing, and text runs through it.
- If an idea doesn't match the emotional filter, it gets cut.

This is not about describing the song conceptually. It's about the *feeling* someone gets when they hear it — the same feeling your content should replicate before they even press play.

---

## Step 2: Match the Context

**Ask:** Where would someone listen to this song?

- Late-night drive? Gym? Bedroom? Party? Walk alone? Getting ready?
- Pick one location that matches that context and **shoot everything there.**
- The videos should feel *instantly believable* — when someone sees your content, it should feel like the natural environment for that song.

This is why Stafford shoots in one location per shoot. The context consistency creates a coherent world that viewers recognize without thinking about it.

---

## Step 3: Minimal Prep Only

- Bring a couple of **reference videos** (examples that capture the visual energy you want)
- Bring a **quick mood board** — lighting references, color palette, energy level
- That's it. No giant creative decks. No over-planned shot lists.
- The goal is **speed and reps**, not perfect preparation.

When you over-prepare, you spend energy on logistics instead of performance. The content from a loose, high-energy shoot beats a stiff, over-planned one every time.

---

## Step 4: Multiple Looks from One Location

The entire volume system comes from this:

- Switch **angles** (front, side, high, low, wide, close)
- Change **where you stand or sit** within the same location
- Change **framing** (phone on tripod vs. handheld; center frame vs. rule-of-thirds)
- Add **simple props** (the orange, the tambourine — props that become part of your world)

> **Case study:** Sarah Beth — 3 different reels that each hit 1M views. All from the same shoot. All by moving the camera to a different placement.

The key insight: **variety comes from options, not from chasing the perfect shot.** Most artists assume more views requires more concepts. Stafford proves it requires more angles.

---

## Step 5: Shoot Full Takes (Every Time)

Always record **complete takes of the entire song** from each angle and each look.

This is the mechanism that makes 50 reels possible. You don't shoot clips — you shoot full performances. Then the editing does the slicing.

- Every camera placement = one full take of the entire song
- Every change of position/framing = another full take
- In 2 hours you can do 8–12 different looks, each with a full take = massive raw material

---

## Step 6: Slice and Dice

Cut your full takes into **segments that test different variables:**

- Different **starting points** (intro, first verse, pre-chorus, chorus, second half of hook)
- Different **on-screen text** (hook text, title text, question text, lyric overlay)
- Different **caption angles** (same video, completely different psychological framing)
- Different **edit lengths** (7s, 12s, 30s, 45s versions of the same footage)

### Testing Parts of the Song

This is one of Stafford's most important points — the part of the song *you* love most as an artist is often *not* the part that converts strangers into fans.

- The parts you labored over for weeks are often your worst performing
- The parts that came out naturally and required the least thought are often the most digestible — and the ones that blow up
- You don't have context for what it's like to hear your song for the first time. Your audience does.

> **Example:** Chappell Roan posting the same song in multiple parts — figured out there were only 1–2 specific parts that consistently converted. Before that discovery, she was getting inconsistent results from the same song.

The solution: **test every meaningful section** — beginning, verse, pre-chorus, chorus, second chorus, bridge, outro. Each becomes its own reel. You find the outperformer, then double down on content starting from that section.

## Trends vs. Originality

Stafford's take: **copy first, then get original.**

Learning content is no different than learning guitar — you start by playing other people's songs. You learn the patterns, understand what goes into it, internalize the mechanics. Then you create your own.

Copying a framework that's working doesn't mean your content is fake. The music is the original thing. The format is just the vessel.

> "Your content doesn't have to be so original and so you — that's the music. Just take a framework. It's working."

Once you understand what's working and why, you naturally evolve toward your own version of it — one that's more authentic and more original than anything you could have forced from day one.

## Alt Accounts + Interest Media

**Key concept: We are no longer in social media. We are in interest media.**

The algorithm no longer shows content to your friends — it shows it to people who engage with specific topics. Your main page is already "pigeonholed" into a content category. If someone found you through indie music, the algorithm serves them more indie music — and only sends your content to people in that bucket.

To reach new audiences, you need new buckets.

**The alt account strategy:**
- Create accounts built around a *specific interest* that your target audience also has (comedy, motivation, fitness, nostalgia, etc.)
- Include your music in every video on those accounts, but the *account* isn't an artist page — it's an interest page
- The algorithm routes that content to people interested in that topic, who then discover your music as a secondary experience

**How to think about it:**
1. Know your target audience deeply — not just "music fans" but what else do they watch, listen to, care about?
2. Find the interest category that overlaps most
3. Build content in that category that features your sound

Example: If your target audience likes comedy and your song has a certain energy, an alt account that does comedy content with your song underneath gets served to comedy audiences who would never find your main page.

## Is Posting 50 Reels "Spam"?

Stafford's answer: **no, if the content is real.**

- The old number for how many times someone needed to see something before taking action was 7. It's now 12–15 touches.
- Posting more doesn't fatigue your audience if each piece of content is genuine
- The bigger mistake artists make is posting 1–2 times and going dark — your audience barely registered you existed
- People can see the same creator post 15 times and still feel excited on the 15th touch if the content is authentic

**Important reframe:** You're making your own TV show. Your audience knows roughly what to expect from each "episode" — the vibe, the energy, your presence — but they don't know exactly what this one will be. That anticipation is what keeps them coming back.

The show analogy (The Office): You know roughly what will happen — Michael will do something stupid, there'll be an exchange with Jim and Dwight. You don't know the specifics but you keep watching because the format is reliable. Build that for your content.

## The Rollout Philosophy

Treat content like the song itself has a storyline — and you're the one telling it.

**The arc:**
1. **Pre-creation:** showing you making the song (even if staged)
2. **In-progress:** "I just finished this thing, I love it, I'm nervous about it"
3. **Pre-release:** "Great news — the song drops [date]"
4. **Release:** the actual song drop + performance content
5. **Post-release:** fan reactions, comments, "I didn't expect this response"

Just those three beats (making it → announcing it → dropping it) tells a complete story to anyone who watches even two of the three.

> Reference: Tyler, the Creator's "Recognized" rollout — started by showing the beat on TikTok. Blew up. By the time the song dropped, people already felt invested.

The more touchpoints in the story, the more a fan feels like *they were there* when the song was being made. That emotional ownership is what makes them tell their friends.

## Metrics That Matter

**#1 metric: Comments.** Not saves. Not shares. Comments.

Stafford's reasoning:
- Comments signal the most active form of engagement
- Comments are versatile: they become your next video hook, your caption formula, your content ideas
- When you don't know what hook to use for the next reel — look in your comment section. Sort by most liked.
- No traction yet on your own page? Find a similar artist, look in their comments, find the comment with the most likes about the topic your song addresses. That's your hook.

Comments also give you a real-time emotional map of what resonates. If people are writing paragraphs, that's a completely different signal than fire emojis.

## The Minimum Viable Setup

**Gear that's enough:**
- Tripod
- iPhone (back camera — the front camera is a "lie" that flatters in unnatural ways; the back camera is closer to what the human eye actually sees)
- One light
- Lav mic

**Performance tip:**
- Whatever energy you think you're giving on camera, multiply it by 10. Camera compresses everything. You have to be exaggerated in whatever you're doing for it to read as *present* on screen.
- If you don't believe in your own content in the shot, it will never convert. Energy is the single biggest factor in whether a performance reel works.

## World Building for Artists

**Two types of world building:**

1. **Hard world building** (The Hobbit style) — define every element, every rule, every corner of the world. Exhaustive. Leaves nothing for imagination.
2. **Soft world building** (Harry Potter style) — introduce key elements, leave large parts intentionally undiscovered. The audience fills in the gaps. Mystery creates investment.

Artists should practice **soft world building:**
- Don't try to define everything about your world upfront
- Let signature elements appear consistently: a specific prop, a recurring visual motif, a color, a tone
- Let some things stay unexplained — the audience will theorize, discuss, and feel ownership over their interpretation

> **Sarah Beth example:** An orange appeared at one shoot, became a recurring prop, and is now part of her world. It wasn't planned as a brand element — it happened organically and got kept because it worked.

**Repeatable formats** are the practical form of world building:
- Audiences know roughly what to expect from each "episode" of your content
- The format becomes a signal: "oh, this is that kind of video" — and they lean in

## The Biggest Mental Blocker

Most artists struggle with content for one reason: **they treat each piece of content like it needs to be a masterpiece.**

The perfection mentality leads to:
- Very high standards → very low output
- Waiting until conditions are perfect → never shooting
- Over-editing → losing spontaneity that makes content connect

The fix is treating content volume the same way you'd treat practicing an instrument — you have to put in reps. Some of those reps are bad. You don't delete the bad takes, you learn from them and do another one.

> Stafford's controversial opinion: "I'm giving you permission to stop treating your music career like a business if doing so prevents you from putting out the volume that you need to."

---

## Summary: The System in 6 Steps

1. **Pick the emotion** — 1-2 words that filter every creative decision
2. **Match the context** — one location that's instantly believable for that song
3. **Minimal prep only** — reference videos + mood board, nothing more
4. **Multiple looks** — angles, positions, framing, simple props
5. **Shoot full takes** — record the entire song at every look
6. **Slice and dice** — test starting points, text variations, caption angles; find outperformers and double down
